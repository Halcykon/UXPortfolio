Improving Upstream Data Analysis

Restructuring how petrophysicists understand subsurface data

Duration

6 months

Role

Research Lead +
Interaction Designer

Company

ExxonMobil

Team

Upstream IT

THE ORIGINAL ASK

Automate data preparation so petrophysicists can focus on critical work.

Senior petrophysicists are expensive, specialized experts who make core drilling decisions. However, they spend over 60% of their time on data cleaning and processing.

Leadership wanted to automate data preparation for petrophysicists, removing low-value tasks so they could focus on work supporting critical business decisions.

THE REAL PROBLEM

Petrophysicists often analyzed data they couldn't trust.

Petrophysicists spent 25-30 hours per week on data prep, but the issue more than just wasted time, it was the lack of visibility and trust.

They were discovering critical errors 6-8 weeks into projects because they couldn't verify data quality, pipeline status, or raw measurements upfront. Building automation that bypassed them for data validation would fail.

THE SOLUTION

Reduced data processing by 40% by designing a workflow that works with their mental models. 

We designed a spatial data validation dashboard that gives petrophysicists real-time visibility into data quality and pipeline status before they start analysis. The system catches errors early, shows where data is stuck, and maintains access to raw measurements—making data prep faster without removing user control.

Impact: 40% reduction in data prep time, 85% of errors caught within the first week (vs. 6-8 weeks), and faster site evaluation for acquisitions—helping ExxonMobil assess potential drilling sites more quickly, cheaply, and accurately.

THE CHALLENGE

It is expensive to keep datasets accurate and updated.

Petrophysicists described the data validation process as "herding cats in the dark".

Just when they thought they compiled all their drilling reports, a new dataset would appear and invalidate previous work. Teams kept discovering critical data errors late into projects, forcing costly rework.

BUSINESS IMPACT

● $2-3M per year lost to late-stage data corrections
● 25-30% of analysis time spent unnecessarily tracking down incorrect data
● ~4-6 weeks of project delays due to data quality issues
● Slower site evaluation for acquisitions: data prep bottlenecks delayed ExxonMobil's ability to assess and acquire potential drilling sites


Streamlining data preparation would allow the company to evaluate potential sites more quickly, cheaply, and accurately—a competitive advantage in acquisition decisions.

RESEARCH PROCESS

Understanding the Data Lifecycle (Weeks 1-4)

I needed to map how data flows from raw measurements to final geological reports. I organized research questions into four categories:

People — Who handles data at each stage?
Pain Points — Where do handoffs break down?
Priorities — What quick wins vs. transformative changes should we target?
Processes — How and why is data transformed?

RESEARCH METHODS

WHAT I LEARNED

Finding #1: Simple Errors Have Massive Ripple Effects [CRITICAL]

Unit conversion errors and mislabeled wells don't get caught until weeks into analysis. One junior petrophysicist told me about spending three weeks analyzing what she thought was Well A-12, only to discover it was mislabeled; it was actually B-09, in a completely different geological formation. She had to start over.

Why this matters: Catching these errors at ingestion could save at least 15-20 hours per project.

Finding #2: Petrophysicists Operate Blind on Data Availability [CRITICAL]

Teams waste days waiting for data that is stuck in legal review, already exists elsewhere, or was never collected. There's no centralized system to check data status.

As one user put it: "I've waited a month for a dataset only to discover it never made it out of the wellsite. No one told me."

Why this matters: Visibility into the pipeline prevents false starts and allows teams to prioritize work intelligently.

Finding #3: Raw Data Access Is Trust [CRITICAL]

Petrophysicists need to see original measurements to validate any transformed data.

One senior petrophysicist stated: "If I can't see the raw gamma ray logs, I can't tell you if the formation boundary is real or an artifact of smoothing algorithms."

Why this matters: Any automation that removes raw data access will be rejected by users.

Finding #4: Relevant Data Lives Everywhere [IMPORTANT]

A single analysis might require data from 8+ vendors, internal databases, and legacy systems. Files arrive over 6-12 months with no intuitive grouping.

Why this matters: The cognitive overhead of tracking scattered files slows every project.

THE PIVOT

Instead of Automating, Augment Data Validation

Initial (Wrong) Hypothesis 
We assumed petrophysicists wanted us to automate data preparation so they could focus on analysis.

What We Actually Learned
Petrophysicists need to stay involved in data prep because that's where they build intuition about data quality.

A senior petrophysicist told me: "If I don't see how the sausage is made, I can't trust the sausage. You can't take this away from me; you need to make it faster."

New Direction
Instead of removing petrophysicists from data prep, I focused on making data preparation radically more efficient while preserving their ability to validate everything.

DESIGN PRINCIPLE

Surface problems early,
provide tools to investigate,
and never hide the raw truth.

DESIGN EXPLORATION

Co-Design Session (Week 6)

I ran a half-day workshop with a senior petrophysicist. Using his past projects as case studies, we sketched interface concepts and storyboarded ideal workflows.

Key insight
Petrophysicists think geographically first. They locate wells on a map mentally before diving into data. A list-based interface forces unnecessary cognitive translation.

EXPLORED CONCEPTS

Concept A: Automated Data Validator (Rejected)

A system that runs validation rules and only surfaces "clean" datasets.

Why rejected: Users felt this was a black box. They wanted to see what was validated and how, not just a green checkmark.

Concept B: Notification-Based System (Rejected)

Push alerts when new data arrives or errors are detected.

Why rejected: Users are already drowning in emails. They needed a "pull" system they check when starting a project, not more interruptions.

Concept C: Spatial Data Dashboard (Selected)

A map-centric interface showing well locations, with layered information about data freshness, pipeline status, and quality flags.

Why selected: Aligns with spatial mental models, allows progressive disclosure of detail, and keeps raw data accessible within two clicks.

INTERFACE DESIGN AND TESTING

Usability Testing Goals

I conducted three rounds of testing with 5 total users (3 petrophysicists, 2 geologists as proxies):

Success criteria:
1. Users can identify data quality issues without reading documentation
2. Users can locate needed datasets within 30 seconds
3. Users understand what every UI element does without asking questions

Iteration 1: Establishing Spatial Layout

What I tested: Map-based vs. list-based data browser

Results:
Map-based: 4/5 users found target datasets in 18-25 seconds
List-based: 5/5 users took 45-90 seconds and expressed frustration

Issues found:
● Color coding was ambiguous (what does red mean?)
● Data age icons were too small to notice
● Pipeline status was buried in a sidebar

Iteration V1: Users validated that our map-based design reflected their mental models more accurately than a list-based view

Iteration 2: Refining Information Hierarchy

What I changed:
● Introduced "produce freshness" color metaphor (green=fresh, yellow=aging, brown=stale)
● Made error flags primary visual elements (! icons on map pins)
● Moved pipeline status to hover cards on each well

Results:
● 5/5 users immediately understood data freshness without explanation
● 4/5 users spotted error flags within 5 seconds of viewing the map
● Pipeline status still felt "hidden"

User quote: "Oh, like produce at the grocery store. Green is fresh data, brown means it's getting old. That's clever; I'll remember that."

Iteration 3: Surfacing Pipeline Visibility

What I changed:
● Added a left-panel filter showing data pipeline stages ("Collected," "In Transit," "In Legal Review," "Available")
● Made pipeline stage a first-class attribute visible on hover
● Created an "Expected Data" section showing what should exist but hasn't arrived

Results:
● 5/5 users successfully filtered wells by pipeline status
● Users called out "Expected Data" as "game-changing" for planning
● No confusion about any UI elements

User quote: "This 'Expected Data' section would have saved me two weeks on my last project. I would've known immediately that three wells were stuck in legal."

"The map makes sense because I already know where the wells are geographically. Why would I search through an alphabetical list?"

Iteration V3 significantly improved data visibility. Users easily grasped "Data Age" based on the color palette while data status readily surfaced action items.

FINAL DESIGN FEATURES

"The map view finally matches how I think. Why didn't we do this years ago?"
- Singapore Geologist

1. Geographic Data Browser

Wells displayed on an interactive map with color-coded freshness indicators. Users can zoom to regions, filter by data type, and see availability at a glance.

Design rationale 
Matches the spatial mental model petrophysicists already use. Reduces cognitive load by eliminating translation between geography and abstract lists.

2. Proactive Error Detection

Automated checks flag unit inconsistencies, labeling errors, and suspicious values at data ingestion. Errors appear as visual alerts on well markers.

Design rationale 
Catches issues when they're cheapest to fix (at ingestion vs. mid-analysis). Visual alerts create immediate awareness without requiring active monitoring.

3. Data Lineage Transparency

Two-click access to raw measurements from any transformed dataset. Visual timeline shows transformation steps.

Design rationale
Preserves user trust by making the "sausage-making" visible. Enables validation without adding friction.

4. Pipeline Status Dashboard

Left panel shows all data stages: what's collected, in-transit, stuck in approval, or available. "Expected Data" section shows gaps.

Design rationale: Eliminates the black box of data availability. Allows teams to plan around data arrival rather than waiting blindly.

5. Smart Data Grouping

Related datasets (logs, cores, seismic) automatically clustered by well and project. Users can create custom collections.

Design rationale: Reduces time spent hunting for related files. Mirrors how petrophysicists think about data relationships.

TECH CONSTRAINTS

▸ Challenge 1: Legacy Database Integration
ExxonMobil's data lived across 15+ disconnected systems, some dating to the 1980s. Real-time data freshness required building new APIs.
Tradeoff: We prioritized 5 most-used databases for MVP, leaving 10 for Phase 2. Users accepted this because those 5 databases covered 80% of their work.

▸ Challenge 2: Colorblind Accessibility
My "produce freshness" color system failed for colorblind users (8% of male engineers).
Solution: Added pattern overlays (solid/hatched/dotted) and text labels on hover. Tested with 2 colorblind users to validate.

▸ Challenge 3: Offshore Connectivity
Field teams work on oil rigs with intermittent internet. They needed offline access but our dashboard required live data.
Tradeoff: Phase 1 focused on onshore teams (75% of users). Phase 2 roadmap includes offline sync mode. I documented offline requirements for future designers.

WHAT I LEARNED

1. Domain Expertise ≠ Design Expertise
Petrophysicists knew their pain points but couldn't articulate solutions. They said "automate everything" but really meant "make manual work faster." I had to translate their needs into design principles.
Lesson: Trust users to tell you problems, not solutions. Probe beneath feature requests to find the real need.

2. Preventing Bad Builds is a UX Win
I initially felt disappointed that interface design was far down the backlog, but our research prevented the team from building automated pipelines that would've been rejected by users.
Lesson: In enterprise contexts, preventing bad builds is as valuable as shipping features.

3. Show Work to Build Trust
Petrophysicists initially resisted "black box" automation because they have experienced bad data ruining projects. Once I showed them exactly how validation worked, they became advocates.
Lesson: Transparency isn't just good UX, it's how you earn trust with expert users who have strong mental models.

4. Small Sample Sizes Can Be Sufficient in Specialized Domains
With only 50-60 petrophysicists at ExxonMobil's Houston office, 3 interviews represented 5-6% of the user base. In specialized domains, users share similar workflows. Depth matters more than breadth.
Lesson: In enterprise B2B contexts, prioritize deep contextual inquiry over large sample sizes.

FUTURE OPPORTUNITIES

Future Roadmap Ideas. How Might We...

1. Proxy Trust Signals when Raw Data Doesn't Exist?
Some legacy wells only have processed data. How can we help users assess reliability when they can't verify the source?
Potential approach: Metadata transparency (who processed it, when, using what algorithms) and peer validation markers.

2. Balance Data Accessibility vs Cognitive Overload?
Users claimed they wanted complete data access, yet complained about being overwhelmed. This tension is solvable through smart defaults and progressive disclosure.
Potential approach: AI-powered relevance ranking that surfaces likely-needed datasets while keeping everything accessible.

3. Crowdsource quality assessments?
Currently, petrophysicists validate data in isolation. Could there be a collaborative data view?
Potential approach: "Flag for review" feature that alerts colleagues when someone spots an issue, building institutional knowledge over time.

REFLECTION

“The art of knowing what to leave undone is as important as knowing what to do.”

This project taught me that the best UX research changes what gets built. By proving that petrophysicists needed to stay in the data prep workflow, we prevented the product team from spending 6 months building unwanted automation.

If I could do one thing differently, I'd involve field teams earlier to address offline access from the start rather than deferring it to Phase 2.
