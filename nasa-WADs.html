Modernizing Work Authorization for NASA's Space Launch System

Redesigning how technicians and engineers coordinate critical maintenance procedures

Duration

8 months (Spring 2018)

Role

Research Lead

Organization

NASA Kennedy / TOSC
(Test & Operations Support Contract)

Team

Ames RC HCI group
(via  Capstone project)

THE CONTEXT

Paper-based procedures create dangerous inefficiencies

As NASA is preparing to launch the Space Launch System (SLS), the most powerful rocket ever built. At Kennedy Space Center, technicians rely on paper-based Work Authorization Documents (WADs) to execute every step of hardware assembly and testing. One mistake could delay a multi-billion dollar launch or compromise crew safety. The current system creates serious inefficiencies: engineers hand-deliver paper forms across the facility by bicycle, critical information gets lost when procedures need changes during execution, and lessons learned from field work rarely inform future procedures.

THE ASK

Digitize procedures while maintaining safety standards

Design a digital system that preserves the precision and safety of NASA's work authorization process while enabling real-time collaboration between field technicians and engineering teams.

THE PROBLEM SPACE

Work Authorization Documents (WADs) are cumbersome.

WADs are step-by-step instruction manuals that guide technicians through complex tasks—assembling rocket stages, testing fuel systems, moving multi-ton spacecraft components. Every step must be followed exactly as written and signed off to ensure safety and reliability.

Why this matters:
● A single error could cause catastrophic hardware failure
● Launches cost upwards of $500M and delays have massive ripple effects
● Crew safety depends on flawless execution

How the current process works:
1. Engineers author detailed procedures (this takes weeks to months)
2. Multiple stakeholders review and approve—safety, quality, engineering
3. Paper documents are delivered to technicians for execution
4. When issues arise, all work stops until alterations are approved
5. Alterations require physical delivery to signatories for approval
6. After completion, papers go into filing cabinets

RESEARCH METHODS

Over three months, we conducted:
● 6 site visits across Kennedy Space Center facilities
● 15 interviews with technicians, engineers, test directors, and quality assurance specialists
● Field observations at the Launch Equipment Test Facility and Vehicle Assembly Building
● Competitive analysis of 12 industrial work execution platforms
● Analogous domain research in field service, manufacturing, and telemedicine

KEY INSIGHTS

1. "Alterations are where everything falls apart"

When technicians encounter unexpected issues in the field (wrong part, unclear instruction, safety concern) they have to:
●  Stop all work immediately
● Contact the original engineer
● Wait for alteration paperwork to be created
● Have someone physically deliver it to multiple signatories for approval
● Only resume work after all approvals are collected

What this means: A simple change that takes 5 minutes to identify can result in hours or days of downtime.

"I've literally had to get on a bicycle and ride around KSC hunting down three different people to sign off on a simple tool substitution. Meanwhile, my whole crew is waiting."
— Field Technician

2. Valuable knowledge disappears into filing cabinets

Technicians learn a lot during execution—which steps are confusing, which tools don't work well, what safety concerns should be flagged. But once WADs are completed and filed away, this context is gone.

What this means: The same problems keep happening across similar procedures, and future WADs are written without any benefit from field experience.

3. Paper creates impossible tradeoffs

Technicians work in challenging environments—high places, tight spaces, extreme temperatures. Paper is hard to reference while using both hands, difficult to read in bright sunlight or dim spaces, nearly impossible to annotate while wearing thick gloves, and gets damaged by weather or industrial conditions.

What this means: Workers develop workarounds—taking photos of steps, memorizing sequences—that actually undermine the safety-critical documentation process.

4. One template doesn't work for everything

The same WAD format gets used for:
● Routine inspections (15 minutes, one person)
● Complex multi-day assemblies (weeks, teams of 20+)
● Emergency repairs (time-critical, high-stress)

What this means: Simple procedures are over-documented while complex ones lack the dynamic support teams actually need.

DESIGN PRINCIPLES

From our research, we identified four principles to guide our work:

1. Precision without rigidity — Maintain exact procedure following while allowing for contextual adaptation
2. Real-time collaboration — Connect field technicians with engineering support instantly
3. Capture institutional knowledge — Turn field insights into organizational learning
4. Design for extreme conditions — Support work in challenging physical environments

EXPLORED CONCEPTS

Solution 1: Tablet Interface
Solution 2: Heads Up Display

Solution 1: Dynamic Digital WAD Platform (Tablet Interface)

A tablet-based system that brings WAD execution into the modern era while maintaining NASA's exacting standards.

Key Features:

Step-by-step execution with smart assistance
● Large, glove-friendly touch targets
● Auto-advancing steps with voice confirmation option
● Embedded photos, videos, and 3D model references
● Automatic logging of timestamps and conditions

Real-time alteration workflow
● Technician flags an issue directly in the interface
● System routes to the appropriate engineer based on procedure type
● Engineer can video chat with technician to assess the situation
● Digital approval chain replaces physical delivery
● All stakeholders get notified in real-time

Context capture
● Quick voice notes for each step
● Photo annotations for "as-built" documentation
● Difficulty ratings to flag confusing procedures
● Suggested improvements that get fed back to authors

Offline resilience
● Full functionality without network connection
● Automatic sync when connectivity is restored
● Critical for work in RF-shielded or remote areas

Solution 2: Heads-Up Display for Hands-Free Execution

For scenarios where technicians need both hands free or can't safely handle a tablet, we designed an AR-enabled heads-up display concept.

Key Features:

Hands-free operation
● Voice-controlled navigation through steps
● Visual overlay of instructions in field of view
● Gesture recognition for confirmation and flagging issues

Spatial awareness
● AR markers show exactly where to perform each action
● Measurements and alignments projected onto hardware
● Safety zones highlighted in real-time

Remote supervision
● Engineer sees what technician sees
● Can annotate the technician's view in real-time
● Reduces need for physical presence during routine oversight

IMPACT AND OUTCOMES

Process Efficiency:
● Estimated 60-80% reduction in alteration approval time (from days to hours)
● Elimination of physical document delivery
● Reduced work stoppages and crew idle time


Safety & Quality:
● Richer documentation of as-built conditions
● Real-time expert support reduces errors
● Complete audit trail with timestamps and context

Organizational Learning:
● Field feedback loops back to procedure authors
● Patterns in alterations inform continuous improvement
● Institutional knowledge preserved digitally

REFLECTION 

What I learned

Designing for ultra-high-reliability contexts requires different thinking.
In most consumer products, a 99% success rate is excellent. In aerospace, even 99.9% isn't good enough. This fundamentally changed how we approached features like offline functionality, confirmation mechanisms, and error prevention.

Respecting existing process matters.
My first instinct was to simplify the approval chain. But through research, I learned that each signatory serves a critical safety function. Good design enhances these processes rather than trying to work around them.

The best insights come from observing workarounds.
When we saw technicians taking photos of WAD steps on their phones, we realized the real pain point wasn't the procedure complexity—it was accessing information hands-free in difficult positions.
